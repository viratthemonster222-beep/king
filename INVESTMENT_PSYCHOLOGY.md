# Investment Psychology: The Behavioral Finance Framework

## Introduction

Investment psychology examines how human behavior, emotions, and cognitive biases affect financial decision-making. Traditional finance assumes rational actors, but behavioral finance recognizes that real investors often act irrationally.

## Key Concepts

### 1. Prospect Theory

**Definition**: People evaluate risks and returns based on potential losses and gains from a reference point, not absolute wealth.

**Key Insights**:
- Loss aversion: Losses are valued roughly twice as heavily as gains
- Risk seeking: People take more risks to avoid losses
- Risk averse: People avoid risks when considering gains

**Investment Implication**: Investors hold losing positions too long and sell winners too quickly (disposition effect).

### 2. Mental Accounting

**Definition**: People categorize, evaluate, and treat different types of financial activities as separate accounts.

**Key Insights**:
- Different treatment of investment income vs. capital gains
- Separation of portfolio into "safe" and "risky" portions
- Overemphasis on individual positions vs. portfolio as a whole

**Investment Implication**: Suboptimal asset allocation and diversification decisions.

### 3. Availability Heuristic

**Definition**: Judging the likelihood of events based on how readily examples come to mind.

**Key Insights**:
- Recent events seem more likely to repeat
- Memorable disasters influence risk perception
- Media coverage amplifies certain narratives

**Investment Implication**: Overreaction to news events and poor market timing.

### 4. Representativeness Heuristic

**Definition**: Judging probability by how similar something is to a typical example.

**Key Insights**:
- Extrapolating from recent performance
- Stereotyping stocks or sectors
- Ignoring base rates and historical data

**Investment Implication**: Poor stock selection and performance chasing.

### 5. Confirmation Bias

**Definition**: Seeking information that confirms existing beliefs while ignoring contradictory evidence.

**Key Insights**:
- Selective reading of financial news
- Following advisors who share your views
- Interpreting ambiguous information favorably

**Investment Implication**: Blind spots in analysis and overconfidence in thesis.

## Behavioral Patterns in Markets

### Market Cycles and Emotions

```
Phase 1: Optimism
- Enthusiasm builds
- Rising prices
- Positive news focus
- Increasing valuations

Phase 2: Euphoria
- FOMO (Fear of Missing Out)
- Maximum risk-taking
- Speculation dominates
- Valuations disconnect from fundamentals

Phase 3: Anxiety
- First cracks appear
- Doubt emerges
- Early selling begins

Phase 4: Fear
- Sharp decline
- Capitulation selling
- Panic dominates
- Maximum pessimism

Phase 5: Relief
- Recovery begins
- Cautious optimism returns
- Back to Phase 1
```

## The Rational Investor Model vs. Reality

### Traditional Finance Assumptions
- Investors are rational
- Markets are efficient
- Prices reflect all available information
- Investors maximize utility

### Behavioral Finance Reality
- Investors have systematic biases
- Markets are semi-efficient with exploitable patterns
- Information is interpreted through emotional lenses
- Emotions often override rational calculations

## Strategies for Emotional Discipline

### 1. Establish Rules-Based Investing
- Define entry and exit criteria in advance
- Remove emotion from execution
- Follow systematic strategies
- Example: Buy when RSI < 30, Sell when RSI > 70

### 2. Automate Where Possible
- Set up automatic investments
- Use systematic rebalancing
- Dollar-cost averaging reduces market timing risk
- Removes daily decision-making

### 3. Create Psychological Distance
- Don't check portfolio daily
- Think in terms of years, not days
- Focus on process, not outcomes
- Celebrate discipline, not short-term wins

### 4. Diversify Broadly
- Reduces emotional impact of any single position
- Smooths returns
- Reduces concentration risk
- Example: 70/30 stocks/bonds mix

### 5. Use Professional Guidance
- Financial advisors provide emotional buffer
- External perspective reduces bias
- Accountability to someone else
- Professional can "coach" through market stress

### 6. Pre-commitment Devices
- Written investment policy statement
- Lock-up periods on strategies
- Public commitments to plans
- Written rules before emotions rise

## Common Investor Mistakes

1. **Trading too frequently**: Driven by overconfidence
2. **Chasing performance**: Following recent winners
3. **Holding losers too long**: Loss aversion
4. **Concentrating bets**: Overconfidence bias
5. **Following advice without research**: Herd mentality
6. **Market timing attempts**: Recency bias and FOMO
7. **Emotional portfolio reviews**: Focusing on daily noise
8. **Revenge trading after losses**: Trying to quickly recover

## Historical Examples

### Dot-Com Bubble (2000)
- Greed and FOMO drove valuations to absurd levels
- Companies with no earnings traded at billion-dollar valuations
- When sentiment shifted, catastrophic declines followed
- Lesson: Valuations matter

### Financial Crisis (2008)
- Overconfidence in complex financial instruments
- Herd behavior in real estate and mortgage-backed securities
- Fear-driven panic selling at market bottom
- Lesson: Risk management is crucial

### COVID-19 Crash & Recovery (2020)
- Fear-driven 34% decline in weeks
- Recovery started before economic data improved
- Those who held won significantly
- Lesson: Emotional discipline during crises pays off

## Conclusion

Understanding investment psychology is essential for long-term success. The most successful investors recognize their emotional biases and implement systems and strategies to overcome them.
